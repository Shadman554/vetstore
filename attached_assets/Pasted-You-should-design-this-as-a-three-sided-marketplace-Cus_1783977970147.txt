## Customer app: navigation and layout

Use a **bottom tab bar** with 3–4 icons: Home, Search, Orders, Profile, so navigation is obvious.  The home screen should show a search bar at the top, followed by category chips (Feed, Medicines, Consumables, Large animal, Small animal) and vertical lists of products or featured clinics.  Keep the layout consistent: same card style for products everywhere, same typography, same spacing, so users don’t have to relearn patterns. [shipturtle](https://www.shipturtle.com/blog/the-power-of-great-ux-in-building-a-successful-multi-vendor-marketplace)

## Customer app: product cards and detail

Each product card should show a small image, name, clinic name, rating, price, and availability (in stock / out of stock).  On the product detail screen, repeat the image larger, title, dosage/size, species, price, clinic rating, and delivery estimate, plus an “Add to cart” button.  This mirrors food‑delivery UX: clear information in one viewport to support fast decisions. [stackfood](https://stackfood.app/blog/how-to-build-an-online-food-delivery-marketplace/)

## Customer app: search and filters

To make decisions fast, you need **strong search and filters**:  
- Search bar with auto‑suggest (product names, clinic names, categories). [stackfood](https://stackfood.app/blog/how-to-build-an-online-food-delivery-marketplace/)
- Filters: species (cattle, small ruminants, pets), product type (feed, drugs, instruments), price range, rating, delivery time, “nearby clinics”. [shipturtle](https://www.shipturtle.com/blog/the-power-of-great-ux-in-building-a-successful-multi-vendor-marketplace)

Put filters as horizontal chips or a bottom sheet on product list screens so users can change them without leaving the page.  This avoids Talabat’s “weak filtering” problem and keeps the UX fast and predictable. [linkedin](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ahmed-amrrr_ux-userexperience-uxdesign-activity-7449112944505786368-sY_7)

## Customer app: cart, checkout, tracking

The cart screen should be extremely simple: list of items (image, name, clinic, quantity, price), subtotals, delivery fee, and a clear total.  Checkout then collects delivery address (farm, clinic, home), payment method, and confirms commission and fees clearly—no hidden costs.  After payment, show an order tracking screen with steps like “Clinic preparing,” “On the way,” “Delivered,” plus live ETA using a timeline or map. [geminatesolutions](https://geminatesolutions.com/case-study/food-delivery-marketplace)

## Vendor/clinic dashboard: product management UX

When a clinic adds or edits a product, use a **step form** with logical groups: basic info (name, category, species), inventory (stock, SKU), pricing (base price, discount), logistics (delivery areas, handling time).  Keep labels clear and provide help text or examples (e.g., “Use generic drug name plus concentration 10% oxytetracycline, 100 ml”).  For large animal feeds and medicines, you can predefine veterinary‑specific categories to avoid confusion. [aalpha](https://www.aalpha.net/blog/how-to-develop-a-multi-vendor-ecommerce-marketplace/)

## Vendor/clinic dashboard: orders and communication

Order detail should show customer info, delivery address, line items, total, your commission, and status timeline.  Add simple actions: change status, print invoice, message customer (chat or notes).  Keep the layout uncluttered—one column for order info and one for actions—so clinic staff can process orders quickly even during busy times. ## How to keep UX very easy to learn

From multi‑vendor UX best‑practices:  

- **Simple registration**: minimal fields for customers and clinics, with clear steps and inline validation. [customshow](https://www.customshow.com/user-experience-design-tips-for-multivendor-marketplaces/)
- **Intuitive navigation**: bottom tabs for mobile customers, left sidebar for web dashboards, consistent icons and wording. [shipturtle](https://www.shipturtle.com/blog/the-power-of-great-ux-in-building-a-successful-multi-vendor-marketplace)
- **Consistent layout**: same card style for products, same table style for lists, same button colors for primary actions across the app. [medium](https://medium.com/nyc-design/building-the-talabat-design-system-e94283dda6c3)
- **Clear feedback**: success/error messages, order status changes, and notifications should be obvious and never hidden. [abbacustechnologies](https://www.abbacustechnologies.com/how-to-build-a-food-delivery-marketplace-complete-business-technology-and-development-guide/)

If you apply these patterns, most users will understand the app just by “looking around” for a minute. [shipturtle](https://www.shipturtle.com/blog/the-power-of-great-ux-in-building-a-successful-multi-vendor-marketplace)

## Commission model in the UX

You take commission per order, but that must be transparent in the UI:  

- In vendor dashboard: show “Your revenue” and “Platform commission” separately for each order and in summary charts. [dribbble](https://dribbble.com/shots/27240204-Marketplace-Seller-Web-Design)
- In admin: configure commission rules per vendor/category; show net payout clearly before sending money. [aalpha](https://www.aalpha.net/blog/how-to-develop-a-multi-vendor-ecommerce-marketplace/)
- In customer app: show product price, delivery fee, and maybe “platform fee” if you decide to surface it, so there are no hidden charges. [linkedin](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ahmed-amrrr_ux-userexperience-uxdesign-activity-7449112944505786368-sY_7)

This clarity prevents confusion and builds trust among clinics and buyers. [shipturtle](https://www.shipturtle.com/blog/the-power-of-great-ux-in-building-a-successful-multi-vendor-marketplace)
